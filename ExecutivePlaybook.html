Split-Dollar IUL · Documented 2016
Jim Harbaugh
Head Coach, Univ. of Michigan → Los Angeles Chargers
Strategy used
Split-Dollar Loan Agreement — IUL

The University of Michigan loaned Harbaugh $14 million across seven years to fund a cash-value life insurance policy. This structure let him access tax-advantaged wealth in retirement while giving Michigan a retention mechanism and death-benefit protection on their investment.

Michigan loaned $2M per year for 7 years ($14M total) to fund an IUL policy
Harbaugh can take tax-free loans against the policy's cash value in retirement
Death benefit repays Michigan's loan — beneficiaries receive the remainder
Policy continued accruing value after his 2024 move to the NFL
Result
Made Harbaugh the highest-paid college coach in the country — above Nick Saban — using insurance structure rather than taxable salary. Policy continues building cash value into his NFL tenure.

Whole Life Policy Loan · Documented 1954
Walt Disney
Founder, The Walt Disney Company
Strategy used
Whole Life Cash Value — Private Banking

When every bank turned down his idea for a theme park, Walt Disney borrowed against the cash value of his whole life insurance policy to fund the early development of Disneyland. A loan document from Commerce Trust confirms he and his wife Lillian took out a $60,000 policy loan in 1954 — used to build models and prototypes that convinced Roy Disney and the board to back the project.

Banks declined to finance Disneyland — too risky, no comparable concept existed
Disney borrowed against whole life cash value, sold his second home, and self-funded early development
Policy cash value continued compounding throughout the loan period — the "private banking" effect
Disneyland opened July 17, 1955 and hosted over 3.5 million visitors in its first year alone
Result
The loan that helped build the most visited theme park empire on earth. Ray Kroc used the same strategy with two whole life policies to fund McDonald's early payroll and the Ronald McDonald advertising campaign.

What these products actually do — and where they fall short

Every financial tool has a trade-off. Here is what the research actually says about IULs, whole life, and annuities — the upside and the real risks.

✓ What works

Why high earners use IUL

  • ●0% floor means in market downturns like 2008 (-37% S&P) or 2022 (-19.4%), your cash value does not drop — zero is credited, not a loss
  • ●Policy loans are tax-free — you access cash value without triggering a taxable event, unlike a 401(k) distribution
  • ●No FAFSA reporting requirement, generally creditor-protected in most states, and not reported to the IRS like a brokerage account
  • ●Harbaugh, Swinney, and Franklin used this exact architecture for tax-advantaged deferred compensation
  • ●Triple tax benefit: tax-deferred growth, tax-free access, tax-free death benefit
△ What you need to know

Legitimate critiques of IUL

  • △Cap rates are not fixed. Carriers can lower your participation cap when interest rates change — this has happened industry-wide and limits upside more than illustrated
  • △The S&P 500 price index (used by IUL) only returned ~7.2% annually since 1955, not the 10.4% total return figure most agents lead with — dividends are excluded
  • △Illustrations assume constant cap rates. Real-world caps have compressed, meaning illustrated projections often exceed what actually delivers
  • △Cost of insurance (COI) rises with age — if underfunded, the policy can lapse later in life when you most need it. Overfunding the first 7 years within 7702A limits is critical
  • △Proprietary indices (volatility-controlled) have underperformed in live performance vs. backtested illustrations — real data diverged significantly from projections
Our position: IUL works when structured correctly — overfunded, properly illustrated at conservative rates (5–5.5% vs. the 7.5% many agents use), and matched to clients with a 15+ year horizon and adequate ongoing premium capacity. The Harbaugh model works because it was funded aggressively over 7 years. Underfunded policies are where the risk lives. We will show you the conservative illustration alongside the optimistic one.
✓ What works

Why executives use whole life

  • ●Guaranteed cash value growth — does not depend on market performance or carrier cap rate decisions
  • ●Dividend-paying mutual companies (like Northwestern Mutual, Mass Mutual, Guardian) have paid dividends every year for over 100 years — including through the Great Depression
  • ●PUAs (paid-up additions) allow accelerated cash value accumulation and enhanced dividend base
  • ●Non-direct recognition policies let you access the full cash value via loans while your entire balance continues earning dividends
  • ●Functions as a private banking system — borrowing against your policy for investments, business needs, or emergencies while the full balance compounds
△ What you need to know

Legitimate critiques of whole life

  • △Early cash value is low — surrender value in year 1–3 can be significantly below what you put in. Not a short-term vehicle
  • △Dividends are not guaranteed — though top mutual carriers have an unbroken 100+ year record, the amounts fluctuate with interest rate environments
  • △Lower upside ceiling vs. IUL or equities — whole life is a wealth-protection and tax-efficiency tool, not a growth engine. Comparing it to the S&P is the wrong benchmark
  • △High commissions on base whole life can reduce early cash value — structure matters. A properly structured policy minimizes base premium and maximizes PUA to accelerate cash value
  • △Not optimal as a standalone retirement vehicle for most high earners — works best as one layer in a broader strategy
Our position: Whole life is best suited as a guaranteed foundation layer — liquidity, legacy, and a private line of credit — not as a replacement for market-based growth. The coaches above used it for executive compensation retention and estate purposes. We structure policies to maximize cash value from day one using PUAs and selecting non-direct recognition carriers.
✓ What works

Why high earners use fixed index annuities

  • ●Tax-deferred accumulation with no annual 1099 — unlike a brokerage account, gains compound without annual tax drag
  • ●Floor protection (typically 0%) against market downturns — similar to IUL but without the cost of insurance drag
  • ●Income riders can provide guaranteed lifetime income regardless of how long you live — addresses longevity risk directly
  • ●The NFL Players Annuity Program, established 1998, specifically uses annuity structures as a multi-employer retirement benefit — institutional validation of the vehicle
  • ●No contribution limits — unlike IRAs or 401(k)s, there is no annual limit on what can be placed inside an annuity
△ What you need to know

Legitimate critiques of FIAs

  • △Surrender charges — typically 7–10 year surrender period with penalties for early withdrawal. This is not liquid money
  • △Cap rates and participation rates also apply here — the same carrier-discretion risk as IUL. Upside is capped and participation terms can change on renewal
  • △Income rider fees (often 1–1.5% annually) reduce accumulation value — the guaranteed income number and the contract value are two different figures
  • △Complexity creates suitability risk — many annuity products are oversold to the wrong client. Suitability documentation exists for a reason
  • △Not a substitute for liquidity — annuities should be funded with dollars you will not need for the full surrender period
Our position: FIAs work for the right slice of a client's income — specifically money earmarked for retirement that does not need to stay liquid. We pair them with income riders for coaches and executives who want to know exactly what their guaranteed retirement income floor looks like regardless of market conditions.
